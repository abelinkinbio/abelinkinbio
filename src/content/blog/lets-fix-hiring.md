Hiring is one of the most consequential things you'll ever do as a manager. And somehow, we've collectively agreed to be really bad at it.

This isn't a post about how to write a better job description or how to structure your interview rubric. This is about the decisions you make when you're drowning in work, everyone around you is pressuring you to move fast, and the stakes are much higher than they feel in the moment. Let's talk about that.

## You'd rather drown than make the wrong hire

I mean this literally. If you're understaffed and grinding through work that should belong to someone else, that's painful — but it's survivable. You've been doing it. You know how.

A bad hire is a different kind of painful. Think about the math. The first six months with a new PM are always slow. There's onboarding, context building, trust building — you're taking two steps back to take one step forward. The second six months are still a feeling-out period. And if it's not working? The third six months are spent managing them out. That's eighteen months of limited progress on your product.

Eighteen months is a lifetime in software.

So when everyone around you is saying "just fill the role, we need someone now," remember what you're actually signing up for if you rush it. A wrong hire doesn't just cost you time — it costs the person you hired, it costs your team, and it creates drag that ripples outward in ways that are really hard to undo.

Don't hire not to lose. Hire to win.

## The panel is a tool, not a democracy

Here's something nobody tells you when you become a hiring manager: the panel isn't there to make the decision for you. They're there to give you signal in their area of expertise. That's it.

You're the one who has to work with this person. You're the one who has to set them up for success, advocate for them, manage them through hard things, and ultimately be accountable for their work. Their performance is yours too. So your opinion carries more weight. A lot more.

What this means practically: if four people on your panel love a candidate and one person gives them a zero, don't just average it out and move on. Stop. Talk to that person. Understand why they're a zero. Because here's the thing — sometimes that zero is the most important signal in the room.

The best hire I ever made had strong yeses across the board except from the engineering counterpart on the panel. Their concern? This person had too strong of opinions. Too willing to push back. Might rock the boat.

I hired them anyway. And honestly, that's exactly what I wanted. I want a PM who has strong opinions and isn't afraid to defend them. I understand why an engineering manager might not want that — a PM who challenges them adds friction to their day. But that friction is often where the best products come from. If you don't have that conversation, you might accidentally filter out your best candidates because someone on the panel is optimizing for their own comfort.

The opposite of this is designing by consensus. You keep passing on candidates until you find someone nobody has a strong opinion about. Everyone's a meh. Nobody's excited. Nobody's concerned either. That candidate gets hired because they didn't make anyone uncomfortable — and that's one of the worst reasons to hire someone.

Hire the candidate who gets a reaction. Then figure out if it's the right reaction.

## What you're actually looking for

This depends on the level you're hiring for, but let me give you a framework I keep coming back to.

For mid-level and below, I'd actually argue that less direct product experience is sometimes better. A PM who hasn't fully calcified their opinions about what product management is will adapt to how your company does it — and product management looks different at every company. What you want more than experience is a very high change tolerance. Probe for it. Ask them about a time chaos was introduced into their environment. You want someone who doesn't just cope with entropy — someone who kind of thrives in it. Because that's what PM work is, fundamentally. It's just new permutations of the same random problems showing up in new ways throughout the software development lifecycle.

Beyond that, I check three boxes:

One, do they understand the mechanics of the business — who the buyer is, why they buy it, what the value of the product is? Do they have some familiarity with the industry, the persona, the problem space?

Two, do they understand the technology — not just the product category, but how products get built in this space? Is it B2B SaaS? Consumer? Something more emerging? Can they speak the language?

Three, do they have some relationship with software development? Can they build empathy with their engineering partners? If you're coming in without any understanding of how engineers work, that learning curve is steep and the relationship suffers while you climb it.

For mid-level and below, I want at least two of those three. If they're missing one, I can help them build it — as long as I'm honest with myself about the investment that requires. For senior hires, I want all three. No negotiating on that.

And in any case: if they can't give a simple explanation for the core concepts in your space, pass. Not because they're not smart — but because the learning curve is going to cost you time you don't have.

## The parking lot problem

Here's something that I think is quietly broken about how most companies hire, and it's not about the interview itself.

There's a concept in Algorithms to Live By about how to find a parking spot. The instinct is to drive around the lot and hope you pass by an open spot at the right moment. But statistically, the better move is to park at the entrance and wait — let the spots come to you.

Most companies hire like the first approach. A role opens, you post it, and you hope the right candidate happens to be looking at the exact moment you need them. You write a very specific job description — "Senior eCommerce PM" — dreaming that someone who has spent fifteen years in eCommerce applies. And nine times out of ten, you end up hiring a smart generalist who's never worked in eCommerce a day in their life.

Which is fine! Generalists are great. But if we're almost always hiring generalists anyway, why are we always opening specialist roles and then scrambling to fill them on a timeline?

The answer, I think, is an evergreen pipeline. Keep a general PM role open at all times. Build a pool. Keep resumes on file for six months. When a need opens up, go to the top of the stack instead of starting from scratch. You'll save time, reduce the desperation dynamic, and stop making hasty decisions because you're three months into a headcount that's going unfilled.

There are exceptions — some roles genuinely need a specialist, and for those, targeted recruiting makes sense. But for most product work, a shared pipeline would serve us a lot better than the perpetual parking lot loop we're running now.

## The only rule that matters

I'll close with the one thing I keep coming back to after all of this. It's not original. It might even sound cliche. But I believe it completely:

If you're not a strong yes, you're a hell no.

Don't hire someone because they checked enough boxes. Don't hire them because the panel was lukewarm but not opposed. Don't hire them because you've been looking for four months and you're tired. Hire them because you're genuinely excited about what they're going to bring, and you can't wait to see what they build.

The people you hire are a direct reflection of your judgment as a manager. They'll shape your product, your team culture, and your reputation. They'll either accelerate everything or quietly slow it all down.

Be patient. Be intentional. And hold out for the ones who make you say yes without hesitating.
