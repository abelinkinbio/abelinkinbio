For as long as I can remember, I've had this dream of owning my own coffee shop one day. I don't mean a chain or some massive operation - I mean a small, intentionally designed neighborhood spot where people feel like they belong. Naturally, this meant I needed to actually understand coffee. Not just drinking it, but making it.

The problem is I'd been drinking espresso-based drinks for years without understanding anything about what goes into making a good shot. I'd order a cortado at my local café in Lisbon, take a sip, and think "yep, this is coffee." That was the extent of my expertise. But in the back of my mind, I knew that if I ever wanted to take the coffee shop dream seriously, I needed to start from the beginning. So I did what any self-respecting person with too much time on the internet does: I went down a rabbit hole.

I started like most - YouTube. I quickly found James Hoffmann, who is essentially the hello-world of home espresso, and I was off to the races. From there, I discovered communities like r/espresso and home-barista.com, and I started to realize this hobby had layers. It wasn't just about buying a machine and pressing a button. There were variables - grind size, dose, yield, time, temperature, pressure - and each one changed the outcome in ways I didn't fully appreciate until much later.

## The Research Phase

What struck me immediately was the concept of "entry level" vs. "endgame" setups. The espresso community talks about gear the same way the mechanical keyboard community talks about switches - everyone has an opinion and nobody is wrong. You can spend €200 or €2,000 and people in both camps will swear they're getting great espresso.

I spent weeks going back and forth. Part of me wanted to start cheap and work my way up. The other part of me - the part that picked form over function on a keyboard plate - knew I'd be unhappy with something I wanted to replace in six months. So I took the approach I wish I'd taken with my keyboard build: skip the compromises and go straight to something I'd be proud to use every day.

## The Setup

To get started, you really only need two things: a machine and a grinder. Everything else is accessories. But like any good rabbit hole, the accessories add up fast. Here's what I went with:

**Machine: Profitec Go**

The Profitec Go is a single boiler espresso machine made in Germany. For the uninitiated, "single boiler" means it has one boiler that handles both brewing espresso and steaming milk - you just can't do both at the same time. For me, this was the right trade-off. Dual boiler machines are bigger, more expensive, and honestly more machine than I needed as a beginner. The Go is compact, beautifully built, and heats up in about ten minutes. It also has a PID controller, which lets you set a precise brew temperature rather than guessing. This matters more than I initially realized.

**Grinder: Varia VS3 (2nd Gen)**

If there's one thing the espresso community agrees on, it's this: the grinder matters more than the machine. The Varia VS3 is a single-dose grinder, meaning you weigh your beans, drop them in, and grind only what you need. No hopper full of stale beans. The stepless adjustment lets you make incredibly fine changes to grind size, which is critical because espresso is absurdly sensitive to even tiny adjustments. It's also a good looking piece of kit, which, if you know me, matters just as much.

**The Extras:** a scale with a timer (precision matters), a bottomless portafilter (so you can see your extraction and diagnose problems), a WDT tool for distributing grounds evenly, and a knock box for spent pucks. None of these are strictly necessary on day one, but they make the process more intentional and, frankly, more fun.

## Pulling Shots

Here's the thing nobody tells you about home espresso: your first shots will be terrible. I'm talking bitter, sour, thin, watery - sometimes all in the same cup. The learning curve is steep because there are so many variables and changing one affects all the others. It's like debugging code you didn't write.

The basic workflow sounds simple: grind 18g of coffee, tamp it into the portafilter, lock it in, and extract about 36g of liquid in roughly 25-30 seconds. But getting there consistently? That's the game. My first week was a lot of adjusting the grind finer, then too fine, then back, then switching beans and starting all over. I burned through more coffee in that first week than I'd like to admit.

Steaming milk was its own separate adventure. The Profitec Go requires you to switch from brew mode to steam mode and wait for the boiler to heat up. Then you've got maybe 60 seconds to texture your milk before you run out of steam pressure. It forces you to be deliberate and fast. My first few attempts produced what I can only describe as "angry foam." But after watching a few walkthroughs and accepting that practice is the only shortcut, it started to click. Literally - once you hear the right sound from the steam wand, you know you're in the zone.

## Was It Worth It?

All-in-all this project took me about three months and a lot of mediocre espresso. The first month was spent researching and convincing myself I wouldn't waste the investment. The second month was spent waiting for the gear to arrive and watching every espresso video on the internet. And month three was spent actually learning to use the thing. Sound familiar?

Here's what I'd do differently: I'd buy a cheap bag of beans to practice with before using the good stuff. I wasted some genuinely excellent single origins on shots that were, at best, drinkable. I'd also invest in the bottomless portafilter from day one - watching the extraction in real time taught me more than any video could.

But honestly? I'm obsessed. There's something deeply satisfying about the ritual of it all. Weighing beans, grinding, tamping, watching espresso slowly pool into a cup you chose specifically for this moment. It's meditative in a way I didn't expect. And every morning when I pull a shot that actually tastes good - balanced, sweet, with a little bit of that syrupy body - I feel like I've unlocked something.

If you're on the fence, just know this: you don't need to be a barista to make great espresso at home. You just need patience, curiosity, and a willingness to drink some truly bad coffee along the way. The rest comes with time.

Happy brewing ✌️
