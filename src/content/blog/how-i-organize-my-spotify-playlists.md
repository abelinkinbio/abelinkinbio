I have 13 Spotify playlists. Every single one is named after a celebrity. Not because I have strong feelings about these people. Not because the music has anything to do with them. Mostly because Spotify doesn't let you sort playlists into folders, and alphabetical order was driving me insane. Naming playlists after people forces a kind of mental index — you just remember them. Ask me what playlist I want at a dinner party and I'll say "Put on Jonah Hill" without missing a beat.

Is it a weird system? Absolutely. Does it work? Without question.

Here's a breakdown of every playlist, what's in it, and why it exists.

## Michael Serra

This is the main one. If you only ever heard one of my playlists, it would be this one. It's my largest playlist by a mile and it's where most of my Spotify recommendations end up. The vibe is chill — lofi beats, tropical house, anything with a laid-back, unhurried feel. I put it on when I'm driving around Lisbon with no particular agenda, when I'm walking somewhere by myself, when I'm trying to actually focus at work, or when I'm waking up and not ready to deal with the world yet. It transitions well across almost any passive listening context. Late night work sessions especially. This playlist carries.

## Mark Wahlberg

My hip hop playlist. This is what goes on when I'm hanging out at home with friends, when we're driving somewhere fast and everyone's in a good mood, occasionally when I go to the gym if I need something different. It's not a perfect gym playlist — that's what Arnold is for — but sometimes you just want a change of pace. Mark gets the job done.

## Muriel Bowser

One of my favorites, and probably the most personal one on the list. Muriel Bowser was the mayor of DC when I was living there, which is the only explanation you need for the name. This playlist is all DC-based artists — go-go, local rap, DMV-scene music that you probably haven't heard unless you've spent real time in the city. It's a little bit of a time capsule. Every time I put it on I'm immediately back in that part of my life, which is exactly what a good playlist should do.

## James Franco

This is the day party playlist. If we're on a boat, if we're driving to the beach, if someone opens their roof deck on a Saturday afternoon — James Franco is the answer. Upbeat pop, the kind of songs that make a group of people immediately more agreeable. There's no bad time for this playlist as long as the sun is up and there are other humans nearby.

## Jonah Hill

Jonah Hill is what comes on just before James Franco. Late afternoon, pre-game, the first hour of having people over before things really get going. It's laid-back pop — still upbeat, still social, but with slightly less energy. It sets the mood without blowing it out too early. Pacing matters.

## Leonardo DiCaprio

The late night one. You put this on much later in the evening. Techno, house, the kind of upbeat electronic stuff that makes sense when it's past midnight and the vibe has shifted. Leo runs late and so does this playlist.

## James Dean

All classics. Forties, fifties, sixties. The stuff your grandparents had on. I got into this era of music at some point and built a playlist that I genuinely love pulling up. It doesn't fit any particular social context, which is actually what I like about it. Sometimes you just want to put on something that predates the internet.

## Ray Romano

Road trip songs. Not a genre — a function. These are the songs you put on when you've just hit the highway and you need everybody in the car to feel like something good is happening. Upbeat, familiar, the kind of tracks that show up in movie montages. Ray Romano is pure morale. The playlist exists entirely to make the first 45 minutes of any road trip feel like the opening scene of a good film.

## Jeb Bush

This is my nineties pop playlist. Nineties summer hits. The obvious Jeb connection here is that he was the governor of Florida — the most nineties state — during most of my formative years. The logic tracks if you squint. This is an unashamed, pure-nostalgia playlist and one of my personal favorites. Zero irony.

## Bam Margera

Nineties and early 2000s punk and emo. This one names itself. If you grew up watching Jackass and CKY and absorbing everything associated with that era of skateboarding and MTV, you understand exactly what's on this playlist without me listing a single song. It holds up more than I expected.

## Arnold Schwarzenegger

The gym playlist. High tempo, high beats-per-minute, mostly hip hop with some other high-energy stuff mixed in. Arnold is purely functional. I don't put this on anywhere else. You know exactly what you're getting.

## Matthew McConaughey

Country classics and modern country crossover. The McConaughey connection practically writes itself — Texas, charm, a certain unhurried confidence. This has the old stuff and the newer stuff that blends country with pop and folk. It's become one of my favorites to put on at home when I'm not sure what else to play.

## Kanye West

It's Kanye West.

---

That's all thirteen. The system isn't perfect — Spotify still won't let me organize them into folders, which is why I built Bosworth to help manage them — but the celebrity naming convention has genuinely stuck. If you're building a playlist library and struggling to keep it organized, I'd recommend finding your own weird indexing scheme. Alphabetical by artist makes no sense. Alphabetical by vibe, apparently, does.

Happy listening ✌️
