# Tools I'm Using 🛠️

**A running list of what I actually use and why**

I'm not a tools guy in the traditional sense. I don't have strong opinions about most software — I just want things to work, look good, and get out of my way. But over the past year or so, I've stumbled into a stack of tools that I genuinely enjoy using. Not because they're trendy or because someone on Twitter told me to try them. Because they each solve a very specific problem that was annoying me.

So here's the list. No affiliate links. No sponsorships. Just stuff I use almost every day and would be mildly upset if it disappeared.

## Claude

I don't really know how to code. I know, I know — I work at a tech company and I have a blog and I've built a handful of web apps. But I haven't written code since college, and even then it was mostly copy-pasting from Stack Overflow and praying. What's changed is that I now have a thinking partner that never gets tired of my half-baked ideas.

Claude is my rubber duck. When I have a loose idea for a project or I'm trying to think through a problem at work, I talk to Claude first. Not to get the answer — but to figure out if I'm even asking the right question. It's like having a coworker who's always available, never judges you for not knowing what a hook is, and somehow remembers that thing you mentioned three weeks ago about wanting to redesign your color palette. I use it more for thinking than I do for building, which I think is the part most people miss.

## Windsurf

If Claude is the thinking, Windsurf is the doing. It's an IDE — basically a code editor — but one that's built for people like me who are vibe coding their way through life. I open Windsurf when I have a real project to work on: a new page for my site, a Cloudflare Worker, whatever I'm tinkering with that week. It gives me the structure of a proper development environment without requiring me to actually be a proper developer. I can write prompts, see my code, preview changes, and ship things without ever leaving the app. It's the closest thing I've found to a creative studio for someone who designs with code but doesn't really write it.

## Opencode

Opencode is Windsurf's quieter sibling. It lives in my terminal and handles the quick stuff — when I need to run a command, debug something small, or make a fast change without spinning up an entire IDE. If Windsurf is the workshop, Opencode is the Swiss Army knife in my pocket. Same idea, different scale. Together they cover about 95% of what I need to build things.

## Superwhisper

This one is new and already indispensable. Superwhisper is a voice-to-text app that runs locally on my Mac, and the main reason I use it is because I now have a newborn. My hands are full — literally — for most of the day. Between holding the baby, walking the dog, and trying to respond to Slack messages before my coffee gets cold, I needed a way to get thoughts out of my head without typing. Superwhisper lets me talk into my laptop and it transcribes everything with scary accuracy. I use it for messages, notes, even drafting blog posts like this one. It's one of those tools where you wonder how you ever lived without it, and then you remember that you used to have two free hands.

## Spotify (and Bosworth)

Spotify is Spotify. Everyone uses it. But I'm including it here because of Bosworth — a little app I built to sort and organize my playlists. The problem it solves is simple: I have way too many playlists and Spotify's native organization is basically non-existent. Bosworth gives me a cleaner way to manage them. It's a small project but it's one of those things I built for myself and now use constantly, which is the best kind of project.

## Google Workspace

I use Google Workspace for two things specifically: running PRNT and Google Maps. PRNT is an app I've been building, and Google Workspace handles the backend stuff — docs, sheets, the boring-but-necessary infrastructure that keeps a project organized. Google Maps powers the location features. Nothing glamorous here. It just works and it's free-ish and that's enough.

## Pinterest

This one surprises people. But if you care about design — and I really care about design — Pinterest is unmatched for visual inspiration. When I'm starting a new project, I don't open Figma first. I open Pinterest. I'll spend an hour collecting references, color palettes, layout ideas, typography pairings, and mood boards before I write a single line of code. The design always comes first for me. Pinterest is where that process starts. It's basically my creative brief generator.

---

That's the list for now. It'll change — these things always do. But if you're building stuff on the side, especially if you're not a developer by trade, this stack might be worth a look. The common thread across all of them is that they let me focus on the creative part and handle the hard part for me. And honestly, that's all I've ever wanted from my tools.

Happy building ✌️
