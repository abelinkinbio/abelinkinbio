I think about my homescreen more than I should. I've reorganized it probably a dozen times in the last two years — each time convinced I'd finally figured it out, each time wrong. But somewhere in that cycle of moving app icons around like a lunatic, I landed on a system that actually makes sense. At least to me.

The organizing principle is pretty simple: each screen has a job. When I unlock my phone, I should never have to go hunting for something. The app I need should live exactly where my brain already expects it to be. Every time I've broken that rule — grouping things by aesthetics instead of use case, or burying important apps three folders deep because they didn't "match" — I've regretted it within a week.

So here's how it breaks down.

## Lock Screen

Date, time, weather, Spotify, Gmail, Claude, Google Translate. Double tap is flashlight. Triple tap opens the AI camera. Quick action goes straight to the camera.

The lock screen is the most underrated real estate on your phone. Mine does actual work before I've even authenticated. Weather and the date are passive — I glance at them constantly without thinking about it. The rest are apps I want access to in under two seconds. Google Translate earns its spot because I live in Lisbon. The difference between one tap and three taps doesn't sound like much until you're trying to read a utility bill in Portuguese and your daughter is crying in the background.

## Screen 1: The Must Haves

Spotify widget, calendar, messages, phone, mail, maps, camera, notes, a favorite photos widget, weekly weather forecast widget.

This is the screen that does the most work. If I had to rebuild my phone from scratch tomorrow, this is the screen I'd set up first. Everything here is either showing me useful information at a glance or routing me somewhere I go multiple times a day. The two widgets — favorite photos and weather — are the ones I actually look at consistently, which is a higher bar than it sounds. Most widgets feel useful until you realize you've been swiping past them for six months.

Notes deserves a mention here. I still think Notes is one of the most underrated apps on iOS. It's fast, it syncs, it doesn't try to do too much, and it's always there. I've tried every productivity app with a fancy editor and a waitlist and I always come back to Notes.

## Screen 2: Work Essentials

Gmail widget, Google Meet widget, Instagram, Pinterest, X, PRNT, LinkedIn, Goodreads, Claude, Gemini, Superwhisper, ChatGPT, Office Pass, Bolt.

This is where it gets a little crowded, but that's by design. Screen 2 is a direct reflection of my actual workday — and my workday involves a lot of switching between contexts. The AI tools alone take up four spots, which sounds excessive until you actually use them and realize each one has a slightly different thing it's good at. Claude is where I think. Gemini is useful when I'm deep in Google Workspace stuff. Superwhisper is something I've started relying on more than I expected — voice-to-text that's good enough to replace typing for most short-form things, which matters a lot now that I have a baby in one arm most of the day.

PRNT is on here because I built it and I genuinely use it. That's the best possible outcome for a side project.

## Screen 3: Reading and Rabbit Holes

Pinterest widget, news widget, Discord, Substack, Reddit, The New Yorker, The Atlantic, WSJ.

I resisted calling this "backup socials" because that makes it sound like I check these apps out of obligation. The framing I actually like is: these are the apps I open when I have a few minutes and want to read something, not scroll something. There's a real difference. Pinterest is in here because I care about design and Pinterest is genuinely the best inspiration tool that exists — I don't understand why more people in tech don't take it seriously. The three publications (New Yorker, Atlantic, WSJ) are here because I actually pay for them and I'd like to get my money's worth.

## Screen 4: The Long Tail

Bank apps, investment apps, baby monitor, Chrono24, Strava, YouTube, StockX, GOAT.

This is the miscellaneous drawer. Everything on screen four is something I need occasionally but definitely not constantly. The baby monitor is the obvious exception — it's here so I can pull it up fast without thinking. Everything else is low-urgency. Chrono24 is a watch marketplace and I have no business opening it as often as I do. I'm aware of this. It's still on screen four.

## Focus Modes

I use three Focus modes — Work, Personal, and Sleep — and they've actually changed how I move through the day in a small but real way. Work mode collapses my view down to screens one and two. Personal opens everything up. Sleep is just the clock.

The thing that makes Focus Modes actually work versus just being a nice idea is automation. I have them set to trigger by location and time of day, so most of the time they just switch on without me doing anything. The less friction there is to turning them on, the more you actually use them. If you have to manually activate it every morning, you'll stop doing it within a week.

## Two Shortcuts Worth Having

I've built a handful of iOS Shortcuts over the years and discarded most of them — they're usually more clever than they are useful. But two have stuck.

The first adds a quick note to a shared list my wife and I keep. One tap, no app opens, the note is there. We use it for groceries, baby stuff, random things we don't want to forget. The kind of small friction that you'd only notice if it disappeared.

The second pulls up a Spotify radio station based on the last song I played. Not a genre, not a playlist — the actual last song, seeded automatically. I use it almost every morning and I've never once been mad about where it ended up.
