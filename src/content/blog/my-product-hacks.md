# Product Hacks 🛠️

**A short list of things I wish someone told me earlier**

I've been in product for a while now. Long enough to know that most of the things that actually make you good at this job aren't written in any PM framework or career ladder doc. They're small habits. Repetitive, sometimes tedious things that compound over time and quietly separate the people who are effective from the people who are just busy.

This isn't a guide. It's more like a cheat sheet — a handful of hacks I've picked up over the years that I keep coming back to. Some of them might seem obvious. If so, good. You're probably already doing them. But if even one of these makes you pause and think "I should really start doing that," then this was worth writing.

Here are my top ten or so.

## Use your own product

There's a big difference between knowing your product is capable of doing something and knowing how to configure it to do that thing in practice. Shared demo environments make us lazy. They're fine for your first week, but the return depreciates fast. Set up your own account. Go through the onboarding flow. Hit the same walls your customers hit. The goal isn't just familiarity — it's empathy. If a customer asks you to share your screen and walk through a configuration workflow and you're fumbling around in a communal sandbox, you're not a trusted advisor. You're just another user tester.

## Fix it yourself

If you see something wrong in your documentation and the fix takes five minutes, just fix it. Don't file a ticket. Don't create a task. Don't wait for someone else to prioritize it. Most companies have made it easier than ever to contribute to their own docs, and yet people still default to opening an issue and moving on. The issue sits in a queue for days. Meanwhile, the customer is still confused. Default to action. Especially on the small stuff.

## Build a system for customer pain

One of the questions you should ask yourself regularly is: what's not working? There's no better signal than active escalations. When a support ticket gets escalated, it's often early evidence that something is broken — in your people, your process, or your technology. All three of those are things you want to know about. Find your company's equivalent of a support queue and build a filter. Subscribe to it. Make it show up in your inbox automatically. Don't wait to stumble into these problems. Go find them.

## Track what customers want next

Another question worth asking on repeat: what do customers want my product to do that it can't today? Whatever system your company uses to capture feature requests — Salesforce, Productboard, a spreadsheet held together by good intentions — build a report around it. Review it regularly. Have good hygiene. Don't let requests pile up in a generic "Misc" bucket. Create a simple taxonomy. Sort by volume and potential value. Neither of those should be your only motivation for prioritizing features, but they're significant and they should be tracked.

## Bookmark your strategic accounts

Strategic accounts aren't always the ones with the highest contract value. Sometimes they're the ones who deploy in a unique or unusual way. Sometimes they're the ones who push your product to its limits and expose its gaps. Whatever tool you use to track customers — whether it's a CRM, an internal BI tool, or a bookmarks folder — find a way to "follow" the accounts that teach you something. Check in on them regularly. These are the customers who make your product better.

## Get your troubleshooting workflow on rails

Simply by being present in standups every day (oh yeah — go to standup), you'll have more access to the sources of truth than most people at the company. That access means you'll learn where all the skeletons are. "The error log says this but it actually means that." That kind of knowledge comes with responsibility. The thorniest escalations will eventually land on your desk. The earlier you figure out your support workflow — your dashboards, your diagnostic tools, your go-to people — the more value you'll add and the more time you'll save.

## Prepare for the worst

An incident is declared when a product consistently fails to perform a core operation. They aren't necessarily a bad thing. If you're pushing the limits, they're inevitable. But you want to be prepared. That means they're called early, communicated clearly, commanded effectively, and followed up on retrospectively to prevent future instances. Have a playbook ready before you need one. Document each of your services and the impact of degradation. Pre-write your status page language. Have your escalation paths mapped. Chances are these things will happen at the most inopportune time, so automate what you can.

## Dogfood aggressively

If your company has a free plan — use it. Not to demo. To actually use it. Better yet, get your internal teams to use it before customers do. Security teams, IT teams, support teams — they're usually happy to get early access and provide honest feedback. For phased rollouts, start with internal users and free accounts first. The risk is lower and the signal is louder. Your most vocal users will always be the ones who got the product for free and had something break.

## Think out loud

By design, product is single-threaded. That means sometimes the only place an answer lives is in your head. And if you don't have the answer, chances are no one else does either. This is almost always preventable by getting your thoughts on paper. If you've been asked the same question three or more times, write a wiki. If you're researching something new, document it as you go. If you're making a contentious decision and want to be able to recall the context later, write it down. Future you will thank present you.

## Measure what matters

Whether your team runs on OKRs or vibes, understanding what success looks like for your product is key. You want to know if what you're building is actually driving the metrics you care about up and to the right. This doesn't have to be a huge effort either. Templatize a way to consistently pull your key charts and graphs. The easier it is to check, the more often you'll actually check.

## Take a 101 class on your partners' workflows

This goes back to the xkcd comic about efficiency. If you spend 30 minutes understanding how your design, engineering, or sales counterparts work — their tools, their processes, their pain points — you'll save hours down the road. More importantly, it creates empathy. You'll be a better partner, and you'll be able to solve small problems yourself instead of waiting on someone else. Every hour you invest in understanding the people around you pays dividends in trust and speed.

## And always be recruiting

Plan for success. If you continue to grow at this rate, what would you need? If that's headcount, how would you use them? It may happen or it may not, but it's hard to act quickly without a plan. Be a part of the community. Go to events. Follow back on socials. Get ahead.

---

That's the list. I'm sure there are things I missed — there always are. But these are the ones that have stuck with me. The common thread is that none of them are particularly hard. They just require intention. And in a role where it's really easy to be reactive, a little intention goes a long way.

Happy hacking ✌️
