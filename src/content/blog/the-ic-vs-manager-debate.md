I get asked about this a lot. Like, a lot a lot. I was one of the first PMs on our team to make the jump from individual contributor to manager, and because I have a reputation for being (probably too) transparent, my colleagues tend to come to me when they're thinking about making the same move. At this point I've had the conversation enough times that I've developed some real conviction about when it makes sense — and more importantly, when it doesn't.

So here it is. The way I think about it.

## Agency vs Authority

Early in my career, I confused agency with authority. I thought the only way to have agency — to be close to the decisions, close to the problems, close to the customer — was to collect authority. Get the title. Get the direct reports. Get the seat at the table. And then everything else would follow.

What I've realized is that's just not true. At least not in product management.

Agency is access. It's proximity to the information that matters. Being in the room. Being close to the source. That's it. Authority, on the other hand, is something else entirely. It's the illusion of the ability to make those decisions well. Having a title doesn't mean you'll make better decisions. It just means people expect you to.

Here's the thing: in product, you're not being pulled into conversations because of your general awareness. You're being pulled in because of your specialization. And that specialization can come from either path. Both ICs and managers can build agency. Both can get closer to the problems that need to be solved. Neither inherently provides more impact than the other.

The difference is how you get there.

## Focus vs Context

If you're a contextual learner, you'll probably lean toward management. I say this because as a manager you're naturally going to be a part of more conversations. However — and this is important — most of them aren't material. They're situational. They help calibrate your compass. Tune your heading. They're input for you to use as signal. But they don't necessarily result in a better outcome on their own.

The IC probably doesn't want that context. They want focus. Blinders. A plan and the ability to relentlessly ship against that plan without distraction. They get pulled away only when it's strategically valuable to the entire company.

When I think about the two paths in practice, the IC path is really about getting operationally excellent. If you enjoy operational things, it's really satisfying to take something like the software development lifecycle and just get really good at shipping — and shipping in a way that your company values. On the manager side, you're not shipping so much as you're tracking accountability, understanding what everything needs to ladder into, and focusing on storytelling. You're the narrator, not the protagonist.

Both result in agency. One comes with focus. The other comes with context.

## Products vs People

The secondary consideration is what you actually like to influence. If you like to influence products, you have more opportunity to do that as an IC. If you like to influence people, you have more opportunity as a manager. You'll get a little exposure in the opposite direction through mentorship opportunities or backfilling products during transition periods, but it's important to know what you enjoy — or what you're better at.

And for what it's worth, the terminal roles tell you a lot about this. The IC path leads to something like a Principal PM — someone who doesn't just ship their own product but ships across the entire portfolio and acts as a product guide to the org. The manager path leads to Director — someone who sets direction for their division and scales the behaviors the company wants across the organization.

Both are leadership roles. Both require influence. They just point it in different directions.

## The misconception

The biggest misconception I hear is that you need to manage people to be viewed as a leader. It's just not true. You need to be impactful to be viewed as a leader. You are what your record is. If your record is fixing products, shipping products, and scaling products — it doesn't matter what team you're on. You're a leader.

I think this misconception leads to people choosing management for the wrong reasons. The most common one I hear? "I feel like it's the only way to advance." And I just think that's wrong. It's the quickest way to end up in a role you don't enjoy, managing people you didn't choose, solving problems you aren't motivated by.

Not to mention — and this doesn't get talked about enough — management success is heavily dependent on the situation you're put in. Just like ICs, or QBs in the draft, it's just as important to be in the right situation as it is to have the right skill set. Managing high performers is easy and takes almost no time. Managing mid performers takes time but is genuinely rewarding. Managing low performers is a complete nightmare.

And the reason that's such a bad situation isn't because the work is hard. It's because a lot of the time, the reason someone is underperforming is that they're not motivated. They don't agree with your methodology. They're resistant to the culture. And fixing that isn't about teaching them the right way — it's about convincing them that it is the right way. Which is a pretty subjective task. It often feels futile. How do you convince somebody that their way of thinking about the world is wrong? You probably can't. And that's a tough place to operate from every day.

## If I could do it over

I'll be honest. I miss the IC side. That's part of what I always share with people who come to me asking about this. I really wish that instead of going straight for the management path, I would've tried to find another way to get the context I wanted without becoming a manager. Because that's all I really wanted. I didn't necessarily want to manage people. I didn't necessarily want to be in the director circles. I didn't want to be in charge of a division-wide strategy. I really just wanted to be closer to the source of information.

I'm a contextual learner. And I felt like I was constantly so far downstream from where decisions were happening that I was always getting a distilled, game-of-telephone version of what we were actually trying to do. The closer I am to the stream, the easier it is to have a pure source of information. And the easier it is to actually calibrate my compass in a way that makes sense.

But I definitely miss the blinders.

## So, what should you do?

When I first made the jump, the thing that surprised me most was how important it was to understand why we did things the way we did. As a high-performing IC, you're probably the kind of person who just gets things done without asking too many questions. That's part of what makes you a high performer. But when you become a manager, it's your job to understand and relay the methodology. Why the software development process looks the way it does. Why we use the tracking systems we use. Why when we write, we write this way.

You might not think you need that until a mid or low performer asks you: "Well, why do I need to do it that way? Why can't I do it this way?" And if you don't have some dogma — some documented, defensible system — you're going to struggle. So if you do choose management, make sure you understand your system and can defend it.

But before you choose at all, try this. Think about two things:

|  | Focus | Context |
|---|---|---|
| **Products** | IC | — |
| **People** | — | Manager |

If you want focus and you want to influence products — stay IC. Get operationally excellent. Ship relentlessly. Become the person the entire org looks to as a product guide.

If you want context and you want to influence people — go into management. Build your system. Tell the story. Scale the behaviors that matter.

And if you're somewhere in between? That's fine too. Just make sure you're choosing for the right reasons — not because you think it's the only way to be seen as a leader. Because it's not.
