I get asked about my desk setup more than I probably should. Most of the time it's because someone spots the keyboard on a video call and wants to know what it is. Fair enough. But the truth is I've spent an unreasonable amount of time thinking about every single thing on my desk. Not because I'm a productivity guru. I'm not. I just think the tools you use every day should be intentional. If you're going to stare at something for 8+ hours, it might as well be something you actually chose.

So here's what's on my desk and, more importantly, why.

## Two monitors. One landscape. One portrait.

**Dell 27" [MODEL TBD] x2**

I resisted the dual monitor setup for a long time because I thought it was overkill. I was wrong. The landscape monitor is for whatever I'm actively working on - docs, slides, dashboards, the thing that has my attention. The portrait monitor is for everything else. Slack, email, the stuff that I need to keep an eye on but don't want competing for focus with my actual work.

Portrait mode is the move and I'll die on this hill. Chat apps and documents are vertical by nature. Turning a monitor on its side gives you more of what you're actually reading without all the wasted horizontal space. It also means I stop alt-tabbing between twelve windows like a maniac, which alone was worth the price of a second monitor.

## Mechanical keyboard

**Mekanisk Klippe T4 w/ Holy Pandas**

I wrote a whole post about this one so I won't rehash the entire build. But the short version is: I built this board from scratch over the course of 2.5 years and 5 hours and it's still my daily driver. The tactile feedback from the Holy Panda switches is genuinely satisfying in a way that's hard to explain without sounding dramatic. It makes typing feel like an activity instead of a chore. I type more carefully, I type more deliberately, and I enjoy the process of putting words on a screen in a way I absolutely did not when I was mashing away on a butterfly keyboard.

Is it faster? No. Does it make me a better writer? Probably not. But it makes me want to write and that's worth more than either of those things.

## Mouse

**Logitech G [MODEL TBD]**

I'm going to be honest - I don't have strong opinions about mice. I have one strong opinion about this mouse: it fits in my back pocket. That's it. That's the pitch. I work from home, from the office, from coffee shops, and occasionally from places I probably shouldn't be working from. Having a mouse that I can just grab and go without thinking about it means I actually use it everywhere instead of defaulting to a trackpad. Trackpads are fine for scrolling Twitter. They are not fine for dragging things around dashboards for hours.

## Traveler's notebook

**[BRAND TBD] - picked up in Japan**

This is the least "productivity" item on my desk and probably the most important one. I picked this up on a trip to Japan and it's become the place where I think before I type. Meeting notes, half-baked ideas, little diagrams that only make sense to me - it all goes in here. There's something about writing by hand that forces you to slow down and actually process what you're thinking instead of just transcribing. I've found that the ideas I write down by hand first tend to be the ones that actually go somewhere.

The build quality is also absurd. The leather cover has aged in a way that makes it look better every month, which is the opposite of basically everything else I own.

## Mechanical pencil

**[MODEL TBD]**

I am very particular about writing instruments and I have no good reason for it. This pencil just feels right. The weight, the balance, the way it sits in my hand. I've tried dozens of pens and pencils over the years and keep coming back to this one. It pairs well with the notebook and that's about as much justification as I can give you. Some things don't need to solve a problem. They just need to not get in the way.

---

That's it. No LED strips. No monitor light bar. No desk mat with a topographic map on it. Just the stuff I actually use every single day. The common thread is that each of these things either removed a friction point or made me enjoy the work a little more. Ideally both. If something on your desk isn't doing one of those two things, I'd argue it probably doesn't need to be there.
